All That Heaven Allows 1955 89 min

  • Director: Douglas Sirk
  • Writer: Peg Fenwick
  • Original Source: All That Heaven Allows, Edna & Harry Lee (1952)
  • Producer: Ross Hunter
  • Cinematography: Russell Metty
  • Editor: Frank Gross
  • Music: Frank Skinner*
  • Color Process: Technicolor

    • * The music that recurs throughout the film is “Consolation No. 3 in D-flat major “by Franz Liszt, along with frequent snatches of the finale to Brahms's “First Symphony,” the latter re-scored and sometimes elaborated. Also heard intermittently is "Warum?" (German for "Why?") by Robert Schumann, from the “Fantasiestücke, Op. 12.”

Questions

  1. Mary Ann Doane argues that feminine-centered melodrama positions woman as both subject and object, that is, the active agent of the narrative (its forward movement) and the spectacle of the narrative (its opposition to movement). How does this film represent this subjective conundrum? Analyze a pivotal sequence to illustrate Doane’s argument and, then, to articulate the film’s ideological stake in either gender or class.
  2. Analyze the variety of “mirror” scenes in the film and their elaboration of feminine identity. N.B. Be sure to take into account the metaphor specificity of the mirroring surface.
  3. Does Ron Kirby/Rock Hudson function as a spectacle? If so, how does this define masculinity? If not, what is his film narrative function? How does this function define masculinity? N.B. Consider Laura Mulvey’s argument in “Visual Pleasure and Narrative Cinema.”
  4. Analyze the specific music choices as narrative elements in this film. What story do they tell? Does it function as emphasis, as harmony to the action and the style; does it function as counterpoint, as an independent discourse to the action and the style; or does it function atonally, as a decentering of action and style?
  5. Tom Schatz categorizes the family melodrama as an intergrative narrative, i.e., the conflict is resolved by creating a “utopian community as reality.” Does this film’s narrative follow this arc? If so, what is the “community” it presents? If not, where/how does it refuse this narrative arc? And, then, what is the ideological resolution?
  6. Critics argue that Douglas Sirk’s “ironic” narrative, his nod to Brecht’s notion of audience “alienation” as a vehicle for the creation of a critical spectator, arises from a distinction between style and story. His camera work repeatedly calls attention to the artifice of the film’s world while his plot is infused with excessive emotional turmoil. Closely analyze a pivotal sequence to illustrate Sirk’s style vs story and explicate the social critique the tension articulates.
