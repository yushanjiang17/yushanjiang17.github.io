Questions

  1. What/who is the film’s POV? Choose a scene to prove your point. What is the political function of this POV?
  2. Consider the film’s repeated use of the tracking shot. Ideologically? How is it different than the long take of classic Neo-Realism?
  3. How does the film’s sound function as mise-en-cadre? Choose a scene to prove your point.
  4. Late-capitalism is a phenomenon that expresses capitalism’s distortions of human life and psyche. Which scene in the film best illustrates this reality?
  5. How is this film similar and different than Umberto ‘D in its use of a dog? Compare the endings: Political comment? Is it more, less, or decentering of sentimentalism?
  6. Contemporary critics argue that whereas the film relies on melodrama—an excessive appeal to the emotions over reason—its plot of run-ins with on-the-ground, minor-key material hurdles decenters and realigns the emotive appeal making it political. Agree? Disagree? N.B. “If you can’t afford dog food, you shouldn’t have a dog.”
  7. How does gender intersect with the tenants of neo-realism? How is the film’s protagonist ideologically different or similar to Nights of Cabrinia? Some critics have suggested that the protagonist is not only feminine but child-like, more orphan than itinerant and so exempted from capitalism’s violence. Agree? Disagree?
  8. Consider the film’s representation of the politics of space as property. How does the issue of homelessness become a narrative? Consider the relationship between Wendy and the Security Guard.
  9. The film is a series of incidents within a twenty-four hour or so narrative container. How is this use of time similar, different or decentering of Neo-Realism’s picaresque? How are they linked ideologically? Is this film a political discussion or an expose?
