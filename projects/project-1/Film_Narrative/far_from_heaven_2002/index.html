Questions

  1. Rebecca Sherr argues that the melodrama’s genric importance of the visual, that is, the style of its objects, its mise-en-scene as truth, “The film communicates that heterosexual, interracial desire could, in a sense, be seen as even more "queer" than homosexuality, at least in the context of queerness as visible deviance.” Agree? Disagree? Argue your position via a detailed sequence analysis that emphasizes a ‘visual’ filmic gesture.
  2. 1950’s Sirkian Melodrama famously exploited Technicolor aesthetic—"bright, bold, and saturated to the point of surreality.” How does Haynes employ technicolor as a surrealist film aesthetic, per Wm. Earle*? What narrative does Haynes’ color scheme unfold? What is this narrative’s relationship to the plot?
  3. How does the nuclear family function in this film as mise-en-scene? To what social effect?
  4. Mary Ann Doane argues that feminine-centered melodrama positions woman as both subject and object, that is, the active agent of the narrative (its forward movement) and the spectacle of the narrative (its opposition to movement). How does this film represent this subjective conundrum? Analyze a pivotal sequence to illustrate Doane’s argument and, then, to articulate the film’s ideological stake in either gender as it intersects with sexuality.
  5. Tom Schatz categorizes the family melodrama as an intergrative narrative, i.e., the conflict is resolved by creating a “utopian community as reality.” Does this film’s narrative follow this arc? If so, what is the “community” it presents? If not, where/how does it refuse this narrative arc? And, then, what is the ideological resolution?
  6. Consider: What is this film’s social project in its use of melodrama genre, more specifically, in its remaking a Sirkian melodrama? What are the political stakes of melodrama in 2002?

* Earle defines the surreal as “The displacement of things and person from their own space and time, from their own relative dimensions and public value brings them closer to the world of desire.”
