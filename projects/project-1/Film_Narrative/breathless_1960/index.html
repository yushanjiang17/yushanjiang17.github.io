Questions

  1. Analyze Michel/Laszlo as simulacrum, that is, not as character referencing real humanity but as marks of “nostalgia” for character, i.e., CHC’s anti-hero, rebel, etc.
  2. Analyze Patricia as simulacrum, that is, not as character referencing real humanity but as marks of “nostalgia” for character, i.e., Popular Culture’s modern single girl; American in Paris, etc.
  3. Consider the film's use of jump cuts as narrative structure. How/what does it configure human ‘action’ and story? N.B. Andrew Sarris, film and culture critic, analyzed it as existentially representing "the meaninglessness of the time interval between moral decisions."
  4. When what it takes to make a film, Godard quoted D.W. Griffith: “All you need for a movie is a girl and a gun.” Analyze Breathless ‘and New Wave’s ethos in relation to this quotation.
  5. Consider the film’s play of gender as story and as character.
  6. Who/what is the film’s POV? How does this align itself with New Wave principles?
  7. What ideological effect does the film’s allusion to the CHC gangster genre produce? ON the spectator? On the film’s pleasure of knowing?
  8. Explain the name in terms of French New Wave principles.
  9. Consider the film's conclusion and its final lines of dialogue. The original French is ambiguous; it is unclear whether Michel is condemning Patricia or condemning the world in general; it is unclear if the Police Inspector Vital is intentionally twisting Michel's meaning. It is also unclear whether Patricia is questioning Michel's scorn, questioning the meaning of a French word as elsewhere in the film, or unable to understand the concept of what Michel is saying as it is told to her by Vital when the two catch up with the dying Michel. Which of the following best ends the film?
