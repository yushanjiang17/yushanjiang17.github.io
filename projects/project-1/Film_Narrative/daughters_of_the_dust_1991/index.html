Technical Questions

Daughters of the Dust is a narrative about the history of space/place and the determinant socio-subject-citizen movements. Consider its cinematographic presentation of space itself.
  1. Where does film disrupt continuity of space, i.e., eschews eyeline match, shot/reverse shot, space give autonomy from plot, i.e, space established as existing both before and after character action or independent form scenographic narrative?
  2. Where is ‘the absent one,” i.e., look of non-spectator, look of other?
  3. Where does the film use “long take,” a shot that exceeds the look or presence of a character’s POV, potentially putting in place the intervals between things/events?

Questions

  1. Consider the island as history as space or place. How does this concept represent African American identity?
  2. Discuss the dialogue of religious beliefs and its function in a modernist African American identity? N.B. Henderson’s essay.
  3. How does the Unborn Child’s voice over situate the narrative?
  4. Consider the thematic function of Mr. Snead, especially his fascination with the kaleidoscope.
  5. How is masculinity and femininity designated on the island? How are these gender categories impacted by and impact on the film’s representation African American identity?
  6. Consider the symbolic effect of the indigo plantation.
  7. Dash was adamant about using the Gullah dialect. What is the effect of that linguistic choice?
  8. Discuss the socio-political effect of the “crick crack” narrative and the occasion of the indaba.

    N.B. ‘Crick Crack’ is a branch of Caribbean folktales derived from Africa: One of the common elements is that they are not just stories, but performance traditions…It is a group performance in which the ‘audience’ participates and there are close connections between performers and audience to the extent that the two almost become one.  It has the African format of a leader and chorus in which the participants are both chorus and audience, participating in elements and parts of the whole storytelling session

    An indaba (pronounced in-dah-bah is an important conference held by the izinDuna (principal men) of the Zulu or Xhosa peoples of South Africa. (Such meetings are also practiced by the Swazi, who refer to them using the close cognate indzaba.) Indabas may include only the izinDuna of a particular community, or they may be held with representatives of other communities. The term comes from a Zulu language word meaning "business" or "matter" .The term has found widespread use throughout Southern Africa and often simply means gathering or meeting
